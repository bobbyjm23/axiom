**Body draft**

Staff already use ChatGPT on client matters. Approval has not caught up.

That is the shadow AI problem in mid-market professional firms. People reach for a helpful tool because the approved stack feels slow, locked down, or missing. The firm often finds out after a matter is already in play — not because anyone wanted to leak data, but because consumer AI was the only thing that felt useful today.

For Australian law and advisory practices, that gap is not a “tech preference.” It is a control and duty problem: where did the prompt go, what document left the firm boundary, and who can reconstruct that path if a partner or client asks?

Approved AI is not “block everything.” It is a path that is as usable as ChatGPT, wired to firm documents, and observable enough that risk and IT can stand behind it.

If your firm is still relying on informal bans and hope, start with a clear picture of the gap — then decide what “approved” must mean in practice.

**Body draft**

An approved assistant is useless if answers cannot show their sources.

Partners and practice managers do not need magic summaries. They need to know which firm document underpinned a draft, whether the model inventively filled a gap, and whether a junior can defend the answer in supervision — not just paste it into a matter.

That is why citations are not a polish feature. They are the line between “interesting chat” and something the firm can treat as part of an approved workflow. Firm documents in; sourced answers out. If the source trail is weak, the answer is entertainment, not operational AI.

Sovereign Warden is built around that bar: an assistant people will actually use, with the provenance trail risk and IT can inspect. Hosted in Australia to start, with a clear path onto your own infrastructure when you are ready — without rebuilding the product around a new vendor.

The shock is rarely the first Copilot seat. It is seat 50.

License maths looks tidy on a pilot. It gets less tidy when adoption spreads across a 50–250 person firm, when “AI features” multiply across Microsoft 365, and when nobody modelled residency, retention, or the cost of answers you cannot source from firm matter data.

We are not arguing that Copilot has no place. We are arguing that many mid-market firms under-cost the path from curiosity to firm-wide habit — and over-assume that a productivity suite equals a sovereign assistant for client work.

On-prem and AU-hosted sovereign stacks have their own cost curve (GPU, ops, change). The useful comparison is total cost and total control over two to three years, not the first invoice.

If your board pack only has the per-seat slide, you are still early in the conversation.
