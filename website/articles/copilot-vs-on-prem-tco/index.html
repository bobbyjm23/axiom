The true cost of Copilot vs on-prem AI

A 3-year total cost of ownership model for 50 users — and why customer-owned infrastructure wins on sovereignty and economics.

When boards compare AI options, the conversation often stops at Microsoft's per-seat price. That's a mistake. For regulated Australian mid-market firms, the real comparison spans sovereignty, recurring OpEx, hardware ownership, and delivery cost over a 3-year horizon.

The four options we model

  • Microsoft Copilot — ~$45/user/month, US cloud inference
  • Sovereign Warden hosted wedge — pilot entry on managed AU infrastructure
  • Sovereign Warden on-prem turnkey — production deploy + GPU hardware + support
  • AWS GPU hosting (DIY open-source) — you run Llama on rented GPUs

"At sustained volume, owned infrastructure is 8–18× cheaper per token than metered cloud APIs — with payback often inside months."

— Premya.ai market positioning (competitor benchmark)

Why Copilot looks cheap until it doesn't

At 50 users, Copilot's ~$27k annual licence fee appears manageable. But you still route confidential prompts through US infrastructure, you still pay per seat forever, and you gain no depreciable asset. For firms where data residency is non-negotiable, Copilot isn't on the shortlist regardless of price.

Hidden costs in cloud AI

  • Per-seat escalation as headcount grows
  • No CapEx offset on balance sheet
  • Compliance overhead proving cross-border flows
  • Shadow IT when power users hit Copilot limits

The on-prem economics

Sovereign Warden's on-prem turnkey model (~$119k over 3 years for 50 users) includes deployment, a GPU server bundle ($18k–$45k), and annual support. Your COGS as a buyer shift from perpetual rent to:

  1. One-time deployment and hardware (depreciable)
  2. Predictable support retainer (18% of deploy, min $36k/yr)
  3. Electricity and rack space (modest for single-GPU dept tier)

Hosted wedge is the entry, not the destination. Our ~$138k 3-year hosted path gets you to production fast. When you're ready, profile-switch to on-prem without replatforming — the pilot wasn't a throwaway demo.

How we compare to Premya and Cetus on price

Premya productises private AI appliances with fixed setup + monthly retainer — strong for SMEs wanting a turnkey box. Cetus sells enterprise governance (Songlines Control) from ~$10k/month on Azure Marketplace — strong for runtime compliance and shadow AI discovery, less focused on employee chat UX.

Sovereign Warden targets the gap: mid-market professional services that need desktop UX and published pilot pricing ($55k–$120k) without enterprise procurement theatre.
